Introduction to Embedded Systems

What Is an Embedded System?

Definition

An embedded system is a computer system with a dedicated function within a larger mechanical or electronic system. It is designed to perform specific tasks, often with real-time constraints, and typically includes a combination of hardware and software.

Embedded systems differ from general-purpose computers in several ways: they are optimised for a specific task, have constrained resources (CPU, memory, power), must often operate in real-time, and frequently interact with the physical world through sensors and actuators.

Key Insight

Over 98% of all processors manufactured today are used in embedded systems — not in PCs or servers. From automotive ECUs and medical devices to smart appliances and industrial controllers, embedded systems are everywhere.

Design Challenges

Real-Time Constraints

Definition

Hard real-time: Missing a deadline is a system failure (e.g., airbag deployment).
Soft real-time: Missing a deadline degrades quality but is not catastrophic (e.g., video streaming).

Resource Constraints

Embedded designers must optimise for:

  • Memory: Limited Flash (code) and RAM (data). No virtual memory on most MCUs.
  • Processing power: Must meet real-time deadlines with limited MIPS.
  • Power: Battery-powered devices may need to run for years.
  • Cost: Bill of Materials (BOM) is critical at scale.

Concurrent Engineering

Hardware and software are developed in parallel — hardware may not be available when software development starts, requiring simulation, emulation, or development boards.

Case Study: Anti-Lock Braking System (ABS)

Example

An ABS system monitors wheel speed sensors and modulates brake pressure to prevent wheel lockup. The slip ratio is defined as:

$$\lambda = \frac{v_{\text{vehicle}} - v_{\text{wheel}}}{v_{\text{vehicle}}}$$

Optimal braking occurs at $\lambda \approx 0.15$–$0.20$. The ABS controller must:

  • Read 4 wheel speed sensors every 5 ms (hard real-time)
  • Compute slip ratio for each wheel
  • Modulate hydraulic valve actuators within 10 ms
  • Operate from $-40°$C to $+125°$C

This exemplifies a safety-critical, hard real-time embedded system.
